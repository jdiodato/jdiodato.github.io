I recently decided to rekindle an interest of mine that I discovered in grad school: statistics and quantitative methodologies in education. During my graduate coursework in the [higher education and student affairs](https://education.indiana.edu/programs/higher-education-and-student-affairs.html) masters' program at Indiana University, I had the opportunity to take a few classes that introduced me to the wonderful world of statistics. In addition, I completed the [graduate certificate in institutional research](https://education.indiana.edu/programs/graduate/certificate/institutional-research.html), which exposed me to a new area of interest as a higher education practitioner.

I thought it would be useful to document my journey via weekly blog updates so that I can track my progress and learning over time.

I want to preface this by noting that my mathematical aptitude is remarkably average. Asides from the introductory stats courses noted above, my mathematical background stops at undergraduate precalculus. The only real advantage that I have in this undertaking is a humble background in computer programming, namely the [Python](https://www.python.org/) and [R](https://www.r-project.org/about.html) languages.

For practical purposes, I consider this week 0, or prep week of this new undertaking. What exactly has that entailed?

* I've re-installed R and RStudio on my laptop, which currently runs a distribution of Linux known as Ubuntu. The [tidyverse ](https://www.tidyverse.org/)is a collection of R packages designed for data science. There are some interesting articles on the tidyverse webpage -- I'll leave those for the interested reader to explore.
* The early stages of researching a learning path. I don't quite have this part perfectly flushed out yet, but there's plenty to be done in the meantime. I'm beginning with a highly regarded (and freely available) book within the R stats community, R for Data Science by Wickham and Grolemund.
* On the more theoretical side of things, my first major goal is to teach myself calculus`[^1]`. I'll be starting with a thorough review of precalculus and trigonometry by studying the [Openstax _Precalculus_ textbook](https://openstax.org/details/books/precalculus).

The requisite prep work isn't glamorous by any means, but it is certainly important nonetheless. I'll use #DioStats as a hashtag on Twitter to highlight parts of my journey as well as a category of blog posts here on the site to keep things organized. Projects and other musings will be filed under other tags, but I want to try to keep things nice and organized. 

All of these things were the easy part. Now the hard work begins. I'm excited to see how it goes. 

\- Joe

    [^1]: I don't want to develop a technical competency in R without also developing an intuition for the mathematical principles that undergird statistics. Otherwise I'll just end up plugging and chugging random things into the software to get the desired output. I haven't fully flushed out the theoretical side of my learning path, but it'll for sure include (at minimal): calculus, probablity, and linear algebra.
