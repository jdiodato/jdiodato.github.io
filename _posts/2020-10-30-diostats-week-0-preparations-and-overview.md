I recently decided to rekindle an interest of mine that I discovered in grad school: statistics and quantitative methodologies in education. During my graduate coursework in the [higher education and student affairs](https://education.indiana.edu/programs/higher-education-and-student-affairs.html) masters' program at Indiana University, I had the opportunity to take a few classes that introduced me to the wonderful world of statistics. In addition, I completed the [graduate certificate in institutional research](https://education.indiana.edu/programs/graduate/certificate/institutional-research.html), which exposed me to a new area of interest as a higher education practitioner. There are a few reasons why this undertaking will prove quite fruitful:

1. I hope to complete a doctoral program one day in a field/area that will allow me to directly use the quantitative chops that I hope to hone through this learning process.
2. Curating a deeper knowledge of statistics and quantitative methodologies will make me a better professional.
3. Perhaps most importantly -- I believe that I'll have a good deal of fun on this journey.

I thought it would be useful to document my journey via weekly blog updates so that I can track my progress and learning over time.

I want to preface this by noting that my mathematical aptitude is remarkably average. Asides from the introductory stats courses noted above, my mathematical background stops at undergraduate precalculus. The only real advantage that I have in this undertaking is a humble background in computer programming, namely the [Python](https://www.python.org/) and [R](https://www.r-project.org/about.html) languages.

For practical purposes, I consider this week 0, or prep week of this new undertaking. What exactly has that entailed?

* I've re-installed R and RStudio on my laptop, which currently runs a distribution of Linux known as [Ubuntu](https://ubuntu.com/desktop). The [tidyverse ](https://www.tidyverse.org/)is a collection of R packages designed for data science. There are some interesting articles on the tidyverse webpage -- I'll leave those for the interested reader to explore.
* The early stages of researching a learning path. I don't quite have this part perfectly flushed out yet, but there's plenty to be done in the meantime. I'm beginning with a highly regarded (and freely available) book within the R stats community, [R for Data Science](https://www.google.com/url?sa=t&rct=j&q=&esrc=s&source=web&cd=&cad=rja&uact=8&ved=2ahUKEwjJyMKat9vsAhVLmeAKHUFzBiEQFjAAegQIARAD&url=https%3A%2F%2Fr4ds.had.co.nz%2F&usg=AOvVaw0n8IFED6Hq0JgGKPsl78pe) by Wickham and Grolemund.
* On the more theoretical side of things, my first major goal is to teach myself calculus\[^1\]. I'll be starting with a thorough review of precalculus and trigonometry by working through the [Openstax _Precalculus_ textbook](https://openstax.org/details/books/precalculus).
* Working through the first chapter of R4DS. I'll save some more substantial commentary on the book's material for once I progress a bit further.

The requisite prep work isn't glamorous by any means, but it is certainly important nonetheless. I'll use [#DioStats](https://twitter.com/search?q=%23diostats&src=typed_query) as a hashtag on Twitter to highlight parts of my journey as well as a category of blog posts here on the site to keep things organized. Projects and other musings will be filed under other tags, but I want to try to keep things nice and organized.

This was the easy part. Now the hard work begins. I'm excited to see how this learning endeavor goes, and I hope you'll consider following along.

\-Joe
