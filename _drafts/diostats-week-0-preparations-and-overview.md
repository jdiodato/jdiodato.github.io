I recently decided to rekindle an interest of mine that I discovered in grad school: statistics and quantitative methodologies in education. During my graduate coursework in the higher education and student affairs masters' program at Indiana University, I had the opportunity to take a few classes that introduced me to the wonderful world of statistics. In addition, I completed the [graduate certificate in institutional research](https://education.indiana.edu/programs/graduate/certificate/institutional-research.html), which exposed me to a new area of interest as a higher education practitioner.

I thought it would be useful to document my journey via weekly blog updates so that I can track my progress and learning over time. 

I want to preface this by noting that my mathematical aptitude is remarkably average. Asides from my introductory stats courses, my mathematical background stops at undergraduate precalculus. The only real advantage that I have in this undertaking is a humble background in computer programming, namely the [Python](https://www.python.org/) and [R](https://www.r-project.org/about.html) languages.

For practical purposes, I consider this week 0, or prep week of this new undertaking. What exactly has that entailed? 

* I've re-installed R and RStudio on my laptop, which currently runs a distribution of Linux known as Ubuntu. The [tidyverse ](https://www.tidyverse.org/)is a collection of R packages designed for data science. There are some interesting articles on the tidyverse webpage -- I'll leave those for the interested reader to explore. 
* The early stages of researching a learning path. I don't quite have this part perfectly flushed out yet, but there's plenty to be done in the meantime. I'm beginning with a highly regarded (and freely available) book within the R stats community, R for Data Science by Wickham and Grolemund.
* On the more theoretical side of things, my first major goal is to teach myself calculus. I'll be starting with a thorough review of precalculus and trigonometry
