## Setting the Scene

*As I write this blog post, the date is January 21st, 2020. The time is 6:30pm, and I find myself sitting at the Starbucks adjacent to the Indiana University campus. As I nurse my beverage, I face a myriad of open tabs in my internet browser. These tabs include various commentaries from the [Chronicle](https://www.chronicle.com/) and [Inside Higher Education](https://www.insidehighered.com/) on best practices for the higher education job search. Almost every other tab contains the job application portal for variety of institutions which I hope will consider employing me. I am starting my first job search for a position in higher education/student affairs administration, somewhat unsure of what to expect. The job search brings with it entire roller coaster of emotions, and tackling such an important milestone seemed like an endeavor worth of a blog post (likely several, given the number of ideas bouncing around my head at the moment).*

I have always been a curious, reflective person. As a voracious reader, it seemed like a logical extension of my thought process to also have a penchant for writing. Aside from serving as a reflective exercise for my own job search process, I would be delighted if even 1 or 2 other job-searchers in higher education find some insight in my thoughts.

## Job Search Preparations 

The first decision that every grad must make is when to begin their job search and how much time to spend on the search. Convention amongst higher education and student affairs (HESA) folks suggests that January is an ideal time to begin searching. This timeline does assume that someone is either a graduate student or current professional that will be available in May or June after the academic year ends. I can't speak to how the quarter system factors into this, as each institution that I've attended or worked at has operated on the semester system. Some folks believe that January is too early to begin searching, and advocate for a timeline that begins in February or March. At the time of writing this post, my (very tentative goal) is to obtain a position by the end of May 2020.

It's no secret that the higher education job search can quickly become an all-encompassing process. I casually polled the Millennials in Student Affairs Facebook group to learn more about how many hours per day folks spent on their first job search for a professional position in higher education. The bins for my poll options weren't perfect, but the results were as shown below.

![MiSA job search poll results](/assets/misa-graph.png)

Like the majority of respondents, I find myself in the 1-2 hour per day block. At this point in time, I believe that to be a completely tenable approach to the job search, given that I am still taking coursework, working on-campus, and working on a few other priorities (primarily keeping my wellness and bagpiping a priority!) For my own search process, I've used this past December and early January to prepare for my search -- more on this in the future. Functionally I used this time block to accomplish a few things:
- Making sure that my base/master resume was updated. 
- Ensuring that I had a consistent header for each page of my application materials.
- Connecting with folks who I believe would be excellent job references for my search. I have a pool of 5-7 references that I cycle through depending on which position that I am applying for.
- Updating my LinkedIn profile, professional portfolio, and GitHub profiles. It's exciting that so many higher education and student affairs master's programs require the creation of a digital portfolio as part of a capstone experience. It really is an invaluable resource that can be shared with future employers, and there's no better motivator for getting it done than to do so as a course requirement.
- Setting up job search engines and notifications for when certain positions are posted for institutions that I'm very interested in working at.
- Requesting academic transcripts from my undergraduate institution.

I submitted my first job application on January 25th, and I think investing the time up front to get my game plan together will pay off as I really dive into the search process.
