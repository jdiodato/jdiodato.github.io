*A brief post on why I love the R programming language and why you should as well!* 

Many of my colleagues and supervisors know that I loved the statistical computing environment known as R. I love it so much, in fact, that I've begun to use R in lieu of Excel for many of my data analysis and reporting needs. And why is that?

## Free as in Beer and Speech

R is a free and open-source computing environment, which means a number of amazing things.
1) The code is freely available to view and new contributors are always welcome. The future of R is driven by a tightly-knit community of hackers and academics who have a great deal of interest in ensuring its longevity. 

## Fewer Degrees of Seperation than Excel

Doing many tasks in Excel necessates that the user wade through a sea of menus and other graphical user interfaces (GUI's) to accomplish their goal. While this can be helpful, the very nature of GUI applications necessates that they strip away from functionality in order to not overwhelm the user.

The full power of R is available from the console with a few keystrokes. Anytime a user wants to pull up R documentation, it's as simple as calling `?<function>` in the console. In RStudio, the documentation pops up in the lower right-hand corner of the screen. This allows the user to quickly browse or search the documentation for what they need without interrupting their workflow. 

## The Tidyverse Ecosystem

It's an incredibly exciting time to be engaged in the world of data analysis, and the Tidyverse is just one set of amazing tools that are out there.

## Reporting Tools 

In my humble opinion, R Markdown is really where the language shines. It really is my favorite reason to use R. Not only can I run actual R scripts in discrete chunks, I can also include R output, visuals, and more. R Markdown is an incredibly powerful tool, and is light years ahead of an older workflow of preparing data and visuals in Excel before copying and pasting into Word. In addition to R Markdown, R includes powerful tools to share results, including Shiny (for dashboards), various presentation slide deck tools, and even packages to prepare a website or book. 

## Final Thoughts

So there you have it! I really do think that R is the bee's knees. But don't just take my word for it! I hope that you'll consider downloading R and RStudio, and giving things a go for yourself! I've linked some favorite R resources on my resources page. Take a look and let me know what you think! If you decide to give R a go but have any troubles, let me know! I'd be happy to help out.

Happy coding, and thanks for reading.
